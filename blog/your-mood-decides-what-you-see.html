your mood decides what you see

By Gilbert Bassey March 28, 2026 3 min read

i was goofing around one afternoon, in a genuinely good mood, when a friend gave me one of those looks. slightly condescending, a little dismissive. i was feeling good, so it bounced off.

then something annoyed me a few minutes later. my mood shifted. and suddenly, that same look from earlier became a problem. now i was holding it against my friend. same look, same person, different mood, different reality.

that's when it clicked. my mood wasn't just affecting how i felt, it was deciding what i saw.

the mood trap

when you're in a good mood, the world looks generous. people seem kinder, problems seem smaller, and things you'd normally hesitate about feel like easy wins. you're not seeing more clearly, you're seeing through a filter that makes everything glow. red flags look like nothing. bad ideas look like inspiration.

when your mood is low, the opposite happens. everything looks broken. work you were excited about yesterday now seems pointless. people who were fine last week are suddenly irritating. your relationship, your career, your choices, all of them come under review, and the verdict is always guilty.

i've watched myself do this with people i love. one morning i'd wake up dissatisfied and suddenly i'm questioning everything about a person who was perfect to me the day before. not because anything changed in them, but because something shifted in me.

the scary part is how convincing it feels. when you're in a bad mood, the negative thoughts don't announce themselves as mood-driven. they feel like truth, like you're finally seeing things clearly. but you're not. you're seeing through a filter so dark that nothing good can get through.

how to live with it

one helpful trick is to stop trusting your feelings as facts. not in some cold, permanent way, but rather, just enough to pause and ask: is this real, or is this my mood talking?

before you act on what seems obvious (especially when you are in a bad mood), ask yourself if you'd read it the same way on a different day. before you write someone off, ask yourself if you'd feel the same on a good one. the version of reality that holds up across both moods is the one worth trusting.

this doesn't mean your feelings don't matter, they do. but they're telling you about you, not about the world.

the hardest version of this is with people. because when your mood makes someone look bad, it feels personal. it feels like you're finally seeing who they really are. but usually, you're just seeing your own state of mind projected onto them.

if someone came to mind while you read this, send it to them. then tell me: what's something you misjudged because of the mood you were in? a person, a decision, a moment you read completely wrong?
