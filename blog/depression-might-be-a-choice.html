your depression might be a choice

confessions of a recovered self-torturer

By Gilbert Bassey November 20, 2024 5 min read

before you get angry with me, hear me out. i'm not saying all depression is a choice. i'm not dismissing clinical depression, chemical imbalances, or the very real suffering that millions of people experience through no fault of their own.

but i am saying this: some of us—maybe more of us than we'd like to admit—are actively participating in our own misery. we're torturing ourselves with our thoughts, and we've gotten so good at it that we don't even notice we're doing it anymore.

i know because i did it for years.

the voice in your head

think about the way you talk to yourself. the running commentary in your mind about who you are, what you're worth, what you deserve. would you ever talk to someone you loved the way you talk to yourself?

probably not. because if you did, they'd stop being around you. but we subject ourselves to this internal abuse daily and wonder why we feel terrible.

our negative feelings often spring from negative thoughts.

you mess up at work, and instead of thinking "that was a mistake," you think "i'm incompetent. i'm a failure. everyone probably thinks i'm an idiot." and then you feel terrible. not because of what happened, but because of what you told yourself about what happened.

someone doesn't text you back, and instead of thinking "they're probably busy," you think "they don't care about me. i'm not important. i'm always left behind." and then you feel lonely and worthless. not because of reality, but because of the story you created.

weaponizing internalized voices

here's what i realized: my mind had weaponized all the critical voices i'd ever internalized. parents, teachers, bullies, society—anyone who'd ever made me feel small or not enough. and then it turned those voices on me, 24/7, commenting on everything i did.

every mistake became proof that i was fundamentally flawed. every rejection became confirmation that i was unlovable. every setback became evidence that i'd never succeed. and i believed it all. i treated these thoughts as facts instead of what they were: learned patterns of self-criticism.

the worst part? i got comfortable there. there was something perversely safe about expecting the worst. about being my own harshest critic. about never getting my hopes up. because if i was already telling myself i was worthless, then when other people confirmed it, at least i was right.

the cycle of self-torture

it becomes a cycle. you think negative thoughts, which make you feel bad, which makes you interpret everything negatively, which generates more negative thoughts. and around and around you go, digging yourself deeper into a hole while wondering why you can't climb out.

and here's the thing that's hard to accept: at a certain point, it becomes a choice. not the initial thought—those pop up automatically. but what you do with it. whether you believe it, feed it, build a case for it, or whether you question it.

you can't always control the first thought that appears. but you can control whether you let it set up camp in your mind and start a family.

breaking the pattern

recovering from self-torture isn't about positive thinking or pretending everything is fine. it's about becoming aware of the abuse you're inflicting on yourself and choosing to stop.

it's about noticing when your mind is spinning a story and asking: is this true? is this the only way to see this? is this thought helping me or hurting me?

it's about treating yourself with the same compassion you'd offer a friend. about talking to yourself like you're someone worth being kind to. because you are.

for me, the shift happened when i realized that my mind wasn't telling me the truth about who i was—it was repeating what it had been trained to say. and i could retrain it. not overnight, not perfectly, but gradually.

the uncomfortable responsibility

this is the uncomfortable part: if your depression is at least partially self-inflicted, then you have some power over it. and with power comes responsibility. you can't just blame your circumstances or your past or other people anymore. you have to own your role in your own suffering.

that's scary. because it's easier to be a victim of your thoughts than to take responsibility for them. but it's also liberating. because if you're contributing to the problem, you can also be part of the solution.

i'm not saying it's easy. i'm not saying you can think your way out of every dark moment. but i am saying it's worth trying. it's worth questioning the narrative. it's worth being kinder to yourself. it's worth recognizing when you're torturing yourself and choosing to stop.

because you deserve better than what your mind has been telling you. and the first step to getting better is realizing that you have more control than you think.

— Gilbert
