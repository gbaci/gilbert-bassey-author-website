how death saves your life

By Gilbert Bassey March 21, 2026 3 min read

we spend most of our time on things that won't matter to us on our last day. not because we're stupid, but because we live as though time is infinite. and when time feels infinite, everything feels equally important. so we spread ourselves across all of it and give nothing what it actually needs.

i lived like this for years. worried about too many things, chasing too many desires, unable to tell which ones actually mattered. then i read Marcus Aurelius. one line stayed: you could leave life right now. let that determine what you do and say and think.

it didn't scare me. it helped me think more clearly.

death filters what matters

Steve Jobs said something similar in his Stanford speech: remembering that he'd be dead soon was the most important tool he'd ever found for making big choices. because almost everything, all external expectations, all pride, all fear of embarrassment or failure, falls away in the face of death. what's left is only what is truly important.

i've found this to be true. when i bring death into the equation, most of what i was worried about disappears. the noise quiets. the desires driven by comparison or status or fear lose their weight. and the ones that remain, the ones that survive the filter, those are mine.

the test i use: if i had one year left, would i still want this? if no one would ever know i achieved it, would i still chase it? what passes both questions is worth your life. what doesn't is likely noise dressed as purpose.

death designs your days

but death doesn't only filter what matters, it changes how you live today.

Bronnie Ware, a palliative care nurse, spent years nursing the dying. the number one regret she heard: i wish i'd had the courage to live a life true to myself, not the life others expected of me. not "i wish i'd made more money." not "i wish i'd played it safer." the biggest regret was not living their own life while they had the chance.

this is what death teaches you about today. if any day could be your last, then why spend it on a path you know isn't yours, on the premise that you'll get to the thing that matters later? later is not promised. the only day you can live the life you actually want is this one.

i don't think about death to be morbid. i think about it to be honest. because when time is finite, you stop pretending everything matters equally. you stop postponing what you care about and you start giving your real life the room it's been asking for.

if someone came to mind while you read this, send it to them. then tell me: what would you do differently if you had one year left? what would you stop doing? what would you finally start?
