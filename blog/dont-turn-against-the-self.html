don't turn against the self

By Gilbert Bassey March 14, 2026 3 min read

i've written this phrase in my journal more times than i can count. on good days and terrible ones. after failures and after the kind of quiet mornings where nothing has gone wrong yet but your mind still finds a way to make you feel like you're losing.

don't turn against the self.

it sounds simple. almost too simple. but it's the single most important thing i've learned about living with myself.

the trap

here's what happens: you make a mistake, or you don't do something you said you would, or you look around and notice that other people seem further ahead. and your mind, almost on cue, starts turning on you.

you're not doing enough. you're lazy. you're falling behind.

it feels productive. it feels like accountability. like if you just beat yourself up enough, you'll finally get moving. but what actually happens is you become immobilized. you can't function from a place of self-hatred. any benefit you imagined getting from being hard on yourself is lost the moment you lose the ability to act.

and the worst part is that the self-criticism doesn't stop at making you feel bad. it warps how you see everything. i once caught myself in this exact trap. in the same year i'd edited two novels, saved real money, started a new business, and built a recording studio, my gut was still telling me i was a failure. that i was behind. that none of it counted.

the darkness doesn't argue with facts. it just makes you unable to feel them. gratitude becomes impossible not because you have nothing to be grateful for, but because your mind has already decided that none of it counts.

who this is not for

but i need to be honest about something: this advice isn't universal.

i'm writing this as someone who is, by nature, a chronic self-criticizer. my default is to turn inward, to blame myself, to assume the problem is me. if you're wired the same way, then yes. don't turn against the self. that voice telling you you're not enough? it's lying. it's taking a feeling and dressing it up as a fact.

but some people have the opposite problem. they never turn the lens inward. they externalize everything. it's always someone else's fault, always the world's failing, never theirs. for them, a little bit of turning against the self, or at least turning against the ego, might be exactly what they need.

so before you take this advice, know which camp you fall into. if you're reading this and your immediate reaction is "but what about all the things i've done wrong?" then this is for you. if your reaction is "finally, someone gets it, none of this is my fault," it might not be.

the practice

it's not a one-time decision. it's something you choose again and again, sometimes multiple times in a single day.

i woke up one night from a bad dream and the first thought that hit me, before i was even fully conscious, was judgment. you're not doing as well as you used to. half asleep and already at war with myself. i had to talk myself down right there in the dark before i could fall back to sleep.

that's what it looks like. not some grand moment of choosing self-love. just catching the thought before it swallows you. some days you won't manage it. that's okay too. the point isn't perfection in self-compassion. that would just be another thing to beat yourself up about. the point is direction. are you, more often than not, choosing to stay on your own side?

don't turn against the self. not because you're perfect. but because you can't build anything worthwhile from a foundation of self-hatred.

do you recognize this pattern in yourself? are you the type to turn inward and blame yourself, or outward and blame the world? and if you're a self-criticizer, what does your version of the voice sound like? i'd love to hear it in the comments.
