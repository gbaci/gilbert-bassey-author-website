controlling emotion using the 'snapshot'

By Gilbert Bassey May 21, 2023 4 min read

most of us go through life on autopilot. we react to things without thinking, feel emotions without understanding where they came from, and wonder why we can't seem to control how we feel. we're carried along by our thoughts and emotions like leaves in a stream, completely at their mercy.

but what if you didn't have to be? what if there was a way to step back, observe what's happening in your mind, and choose how to respond instead of just reacting?

that's what the snapshot technique is about.

what is a snapshot?

a snapshot is a brief period during which you register your thoughts and emotions without judgment. it's like taking a mental photograph of your internal state at any given moment. you're not trying to change anything or make the thoughts go away—you're just noticing them.

think of it like this: instead of being inside the movie of your life, completely immersed in the story, you become the person in the audience watching the movie. you can see what's happening, but you're not caught up in it. you have perspective.

why awareness matters

the problem with most emotional reactions is that they happen so fast we don't even realize they're happening. something triggers us, and before we know it, we're angry or anxious or hurt, and we're acting from that emotion without ever choosing to.

the snapshot gives you a pause. a moment between stimulus and response where you can actually see what's happening in your mind. and once you see it, you have a choice. you can let the emotion drive your behavior, or you can acknowledge it and respond differently.

it's not about suppressing your feelings or pretending they don't exist. it's about creating space between feeling something and acting on it. that space is where your power lives.

how to practice the snapshot

the snapshot is simple, but it takes practice. here's how to do it:

1. pause

when you notice yourself having a strong emotion—anger, anxiety, sadness, whatever—stop what you're doing for a moment. you don't need long. even five seconds is enough.

2. observe

take a mental photograph of what's happening inside you. what are you feeling? where do you feel it in your body? what thoughts are running through your mind? don't judge any of it, just notice it.

you might think "i'm feeling anxious. my chest is tight. i'm thinking that i'm going to mess this up." that's your snapshot. you're just registering what's there.

3. name it

sometimes just labeling the emotion helps create distance from it. "this is anxiety" or "this is anger" or "this is fear." by naming it, you remind yourself that you're not the emotion—you're the one experiencing it. there's a difference.

4. choose

now that you've observed what's happening, you can decide how to respond. maybe you still act on the emotion, but now it's a choice, not an automatic reaction. or maybe you realize the thought driving the emotion isn't true, and you let it go.

the point isn't to always override your emotions. it's to give yourself the option.

the practice, not the fix

this isn't a magic solution. the first hundred times you try it, you'll probably forget to do it until after you've already reacted. that's normal. your brain is used to running on autopilot, and it takes time to build the habit of pausing.

but the more you practice, the more natural it becomes. eventually, the gap between feeling something and reacting to it gets wider. you start to notice patterns in your thoughts and emotions. you realize you're not at their mercy anymore.

and that's when things start to shift.

awareness, not control

here's the key thing to understand: the snapshot isn't about controlling your emotions. it's about being aware of them. and awareness is what gives you freedom.

you can't stop yourself from feeling things. emotions are part of being human. but you can stop yourself from being ruled by them. you can create enough space to see what's happening and choose how to respond.

that's the power of the snapshot. it's not complicated. it's just paying attention. but that simple act of paying attention can change everything.
