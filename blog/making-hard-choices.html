Making Hard Choices When There's No Right Answer

By Gilbert Bassey February 21, 2026 6 min read

Easy decisions have obvious right answers. Should you steal? No. Should you help someone in danger? Yes. These aren't hard.

Hard decisions are different. They're choosing between the career that fulfills you and the one that provides stability. Between staying in a relationship that's mostly good and leaving to find something better. Between honoring a commitment and protecting your wellbeing.

These decisions torment us because there is no "right" answer. There's just the answer you can live with.

Why We Get Stuck

We delay hard choices hoping clarity will arrive. We make pro-con lists. We ask everyone we know for advice. We wait for a sign, a feeling of certainty, some external validation that we're choosing correctly.

But that certainty never comes. Because the decision isn't about finding the objectively correct path—it's about choosing which imperfect option aligns best with who you want to be.

"In any moment of decision, the best thing you can do is the right thing, the next best thing is the wrong thing, and the worst thing you can do is nothing." — Theodore Roosevelt understood that indecision is its own decision.

Every day you don't choose, you're choosing. You're choosing the status quo by default. You're choosing to let fear or comfort or other people's expectations decide for you.

The Real Question

Stop asking "What's the right choice?" Start asking: "Which choice can I live with?"

Not "Which choice guarantees success?" because nothing does. Not "Which choice will make everyone happy?" because that's impossible. Not "Which choice means I won't have regrets?" because you'll have regrets either way.

Ask instead: Which choice reflects my values? Which choice honors who I'm trying to become? Which choice will I regret less—not avoid entirely, but regret less?

Values Over Outcomes

You can't control outcomes. The career you choose might fail. The relationship you leave might have worked if you'd tried harder. The risk you take might not pay off.

What you can control is whether your decision aligns with your values. If you value growth over security, choose the scarier path. If you value integrity over success, choose the honest path. If you value peace over passion, choose the calmer path.

There's no wrong answer here—only the answer that's wrong for you.

The Decision-Making Process

Here's how I approach impossible choices:

1. Get Honest About Fear vs. Wisdom

Are you avoiding the choice because it's genuinely wrong, or because it's scary? Fear disguises itself as wisdom constantly. "I'm being realistic" often means "I'm being afraid."

2. Imagine You've Already Decided

Pick option A. Sit with it for a day. Notice your reaction. Relief? Dread? Excitement? Regret? Your emotional response tells you something your logic can't.

Then do the same with option B.

The option that brings relief—not joy necessarily, but relief—is usually the right one for you.

3. Ask the 10-10-10 Question

How will you feel about this decision in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years?

Decisions that feel good in 10 minutes but terrible in 10 years are usually bad. Decisions that hurt in 10 minutes but feel right in 10 years are usually worth it.

4. Remove Other People's Opinions

If no one would ever know what you chose, what would you do? If you couldn't tell anyone, couldn't post about it, couldn't get validation for it—what would you choose?

That's probably your real answer. The one you're afraid to admit because you care too much about what others think.

Making Peace With Uncertainty

Here's the truth nobody wants to hear: you might choose wrong. You might look back and wish you'd chosen differently.

That's okay. You're making the best choice you can with the information and person you are right now. That's all anyone can do.

And here's the other truth: every choice forecloses other options. Every yes is a no to something else. You can't keep all doors open. At some point, you have to walk through one and accept that you'll never know what was behind the others.

That's not a loss. That's just life. The paralysis of trying to keep all options available is worse than choosing imperfectly.

After the Choice

Once you've decided, commit. Not because you're certain it's right, but because second-guessing doesn't help. You made the best choice you could. Now make it work.

If it turns out you chose poorly, you'll adjust. You're not as fragile as you think. You've survived every wrong choice you've made so far. You'll survive this one too.

And maybe—just maybe—it won't be wrong at all. Maybe it'll be exactly what you needed, even if it's not what you imagined.

But you'll never know if you don't choose.

— Gilbert
