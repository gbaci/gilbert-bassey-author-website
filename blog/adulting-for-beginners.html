adulting for beginners

By Gilbert Bassey March 7, 2026 5 min read

the transition to adulthood is hard for everyone. but it's harder for some than others. and the difference, more often than not, traces back to one thing: how much responsibility you were trusted with before you needed it.

some children grow up with space to make decisions. small ones at first, then bigger ones. what to wear, what to study, what to spend time on. they're allowed to want things their parents don't understand. they're allowed to fail. and through that failing, they learn something crucial: that they can figure things out.

other children grow up with obedience as the highest value. don't question. don't argue. do what you're told. the parent decides, the child follows, and for years, this works. the child is safe, behaved, and on track.

then one day, they're 22 and expected to run their own life. and they freeze. not because they're lazy or incapable, but because the muscle was never built.

the connection no one talks about

i grew up in Nigeria, where respect for elders is sacred and obedience is how love is shown. i'm not here to disrespect that. but i want to be honest about what it costs.

when you're never relied on to make decisions, you don't learn to trust your own judgment. when your interests are overruled in favor of what's "practical" or "respectable," you learn that what you want doesn't matter. and when you're never allowed to fail at something small, you become terrified of failing at something big.

then someone says "follow your dreams" and you don't even know what your dreams are. how could you? you were never empowered to have them. the child who was told what to study, what career to pursue, what to value, grows into the adult who can't answer the question: what do i actually want?

for parents

this isn't about blame. most parents who over-control do it from love. they've seen what the world does to people and they want to protect their children from it.

but protection that removes all autonomy doesn't create safety. it creates dependence, and dependence becomes the very vulnerability you were trying to prevent.

a child who is allowed to choose, and sometimes choose wrong, learns to live with consequences. a child who is allowed to pursue interests their parent doesn't share learns that their desires are valid. a child who is trusted with small responsibilities learns they are capable of handling big ones.

you are not preparing your child for adulthood by making every decision for them. that only exposes them to a crisis when the day comes and they have to make the decisions.

for the rest of us

if you weren't given that autonomy, it's not your fault. but it is your responsibility now. (that's the hard part.)

the muscle can still be built. it's just built later and with more friction. you have to learn to trust your own judgment after years of being told not to. you have to discover what you actually want after years of wanting what you were told to want. you have to accept that you will get things wrong, and that getting things wrong is how the learning happens.

nobody is coming to hand you readiness. the ones who look ready just started earlier. but you can start now. that's all adulting really is: starting from wherever you are, with whatever you have, and owning what comes next.

in your opinion, what's the hardest part about becoming an adult? were you given space to make your own decisions growing up, and how did that shape who you are now? i'd love to hear your story in the comments.
