the story your brain tells you about yourself

on the quiet fictions you mistake for truth

By Gilbert Bassey April 11, 2026 4 min read

in the 1960s, a neuroscientist named Roger Sperry studied patients whose brains had been split in half. their corpus callosum, the bridge between the two hemispheres, had been severed to treat severe epilepsy. the surgery worked. the seizures stopped. but it also created an unprecedented opportunity to study how each side of the brain operated in isolation.

one experiment from this era sticks with me.

a researcher would flash an instruction to the patient's right hemisphere. the word might be "walk." the patient, seeing the word with the side of his brain that controls movement but not speech, would stand up and start walking across the room.

then the researcher would ask him why he was walking.

the patient's left hemisphere, the side that handles speech, had not seen the instruction. it had no idea why his body had decided to walk. but instead of saying i don't know, the left hemisphere would invent a reason on the spot.

i'm going to get a Coke.

he wasn't lying. he believed it. his brain had constructed a story so quickly that he could not tell the difference between the story and the truth.

the interpreter

the neuroscientist Michael Gazzaniga, who continued Sperry's work, called this part of the brain "the interpreter." its job is to generate explanations for behavior we don't understand. and it does this constantly. for everyone. not just split-brain patients.

most of what you call "knowing yourself" is the interpreter doing its job in the background. you reach for your phone, and a story is generated. you snap at your partner, and a reason appears. you stay in the relationship, leave the job, take the long way home, and the interpreter is already there with an explanation, ready before you've even finished the action.

the trouble is that the explanations are sometimes wrong. and you can't tell, because they feel like memory. they feel like motive, like self-knowledge.

what this means

i don't bring this up to make you doubt every thought you have. that way lies a different kind of paralysis. the point is gentler.

the next time you find yourself certain about why you did something, leave room for the possibility that the certainty is being manufactured. the interpreter is fast and confident, and it does not always know what it does not know. some of your strongest convictions about yourself are stories that arrived after the fact, dressed in the clothes of self-awareness.

this matters because the stories shape what comes next. if your interpreter has decided you are the kind of person who fails at relationships, it will keep finding evidence. if it has decided you are weak, or selfish, or destined to be alone, it will write the next chapter to match. you become what your interpreter tells you you are. and it is telling you constantly.

the only way out is to notice the storytelling itself. not to silence it, because you can't. but to hold the story a little more loosely. to ask, sometimes, whose voice is this? and is this even true?

most of what you believe about yourself is fiction your brain wrote in the moment and then decided was canon. you are allowed to revise it.

when was the last time you caught your own interpreter telling you a story? i'd love to hear about it in the comments.
