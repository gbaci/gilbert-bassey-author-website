sometimes, you are the problem

By Gilbert Bassey July 17, 2025 5 min read

we spend so much of our lives waiting for the world to change. waiting for people to treat us better, for circumstances to align, for the universe to finally give us a break. but what if the thing standing between you and peace isn't out there at all? what if it's in here—in the way you're seeing things, in the stories you're telling yourself about what happened?

this isn't about blame. it's about power. because once you realize that your perceptions shape your emotional reality more than the events themselves, you unlock something profound: the ability to change how you feel without needing the world to change first.

perceptions vs interpretations

here's the thing: we don't experience reality directly. we experience our interpretation of reality. something happens, and before we even fully register it, our mind has already wrapped it in meaning, context, and narrative. that narrative becomes our truth, even though it's just one possible version of what occurred.

someone doesn't text you back. that's the event. but the story you tell yourself—"they don't care about me," "i'm not important to them," "i must have done something wrong"—that's the interpretation. and it's that interpretation that makes you feel hurt, anxious, or rejected.

the reality is simpler than that. maybe they're busy. maybe they forgot. maybe their phone died. but your mind doesn't wait for facts. it fills in the blanks with fear, with past wounds, with all the ways you've learned to protect yourself by assuming the worst.

our perceptions and interpretations of events have a far stronger impact on how we feel than the things themselves.

this is both the problem and the solution. the problem is that we're often suffering over stories, not realities. the solution is that stories can be rewritten.

the power of responsibility

taking responsibility for your interpretations doesn't mean pretending nothing bad ever happens. it doesn't mean gaslighting yourself into toxic positivity or ignoring real harm. it means recognizing that between what happens and how you feel, there's a gap—and in that gap, you have power.

you can't control what people do. you can't control how the world unfolds. but you can control how you respond. you can question your first reaction. you can ask yourself: is this the only way to see this? what else might be true? what would change if i chose a different lens?

this isn't easy. it requires slowing down in moments when your instinct is to react. it requires being honest with yourself about the stories you're most attached to, even when they hurt. especially when they hurt.

because sometimes we cling to painful narratives because they're familiar. because they confirm what we already believe about ourselves or the world. because letting them go means admitting we might have been wrong, or that we suffered for no reason, or that we have more control—and therefore more responsibility—than we wanted to accept.

you control your response

here's what changed for me: i stopped asking "why did this happen to me?" and started asking "how am i choosing to see this?" that shift—from victim to author—was everything.

it didn't make hard things stop happening. but it meant i wasn't at the mercy of my automatic thoughts anymore. it meant i could feel hurt without building a house there. i could acknowledge pain without letting it define me.

you are not powerless. you are not just a character in a story being written by everyone else. you are the one holding the pen. and while you can't change what's already happened, you can change what it means. you can choose how you carry it forward.

sometimes, the problem isn't what happened. it's that you're holding onto a version of what happened that's making you suffer more than you need to. and that's the only problem you actually have the power to solve.
